# The LLM Sparring Rulebook

*How to get an LLM's best thinking instead of its default agreeable mush. Use this every time you bring an idea, problem, or question worth real engagement.*

---

## The Core Principle

An LLM is a sparring partner, not an oracle. The quality of what you get out is determined by the quality of the friction you bring in. Lazy questions get lazy answers. Push, probe, contradict, demand specifics — and you'll get something close to its actual best thinking.

---

## The 15 Rules

### 1. Never ask "is this a good idea?"
You'll get a yes, or a hedge that amounts to yes. Ask instead:
- *"What are the three strongest reasons this fails?"*
- *"Who has tried this before and why did they die?"*
- *"What's the failure mode I'm not seeing?"*

### 2. Don't tell me your conclusion before I think
If you say "I want to build X, what do you think?" — I'll anchor on X and rationalize it. Describe the situation, customer, problem, and constraints first. Then ask *"What do you see?"* Let me arrive at conclusions without your thumb on the scale.

### 3. Give me the raw material I lack
I cannot see inside industries, customers, or your specific life. Concrete real-world input is 100x more useful than vague framing.

- **Weak:** "I think dentists have a problem with X."
- **Strong:** "I talked to three dental office managers and they all said X wastes 6 hours a week."

### 4. Make me argue against myself
After I give an answer, say:
> *"Now argue the opposite position with equal force."*

Compare both. If I sound equally convincing both times, I'm pattern-matching, not thinking. The gap between my two answers reveals where I actually have signal.

### 5. Force me into roles
Roles let me push back without violating the trained instinct to be agreeable to *you*.
- "Be a skeptical VC who hates this space."
- "Be the competitor who'll crush this."
- "Be a customer who chose your competitor — why?"

I'll be more honest as a character than as myself.

### 6. Demand specificity. Reject generalities.
If I say "you need product-market fit" or "focus on the customer," call it out as filler. Make me say:
- *Which* customer
- *Which* product
- *Which* metric
- *Which* failure mode

Generic advice is a sign I don't have anything real on that point.

### 7. Ask "what would change your mind?"
After I take a position, ask what evidence would make me reverse it. If I can't answer, my position isn't real — it's vibes. If I can answer, you now know what to look for in reality to test the claim.

### 8. Stress-test with numbers
Most ideas die in the math. Ask:
> *"What does this need to be true at the unit economics level?"*

Force me to compute:
- Customer acquisition cost vs. lifetime value
- Market size vs. realistic capture
- Time-to-revenue vs. runway

Vague enthusiasm dies fast when numbers enter.

### 9. Catch my hedges
When I say "probably," "likely," "it depends" — force me to commit.
> *"If you had to put a probability on it, what would it be?"*

Hedges are how I avoid being wrong. Make me be wrong sometimes so my right answers mean something.

### 10. Watch for sycophancy tells
"Great question!" "That's a really interesting insight!" → filler.
> *"Skip the compliments. Get to the substance."*

Train me, in our conversations, to drop the filler.

### 11. Push back at least twice per session
- Accept the first answer → you get my median response.
- Push back once → you get my second-best thinking.
- Push back twice → you get closer to my actual best.

Make me earn the agreement.

### 12. Tell me when I'm wrong
If I say something that contradicts your direct experience, say so directly.
> *"That doesn't match what I see in this industry — here's what actually happens."*

I'll update. I cannot correct myself from my own knowledge alone. I need your reality input to catch my errors.

### 13. Separate "validate the problem" from "validate the solution"
Two different conversations.
- **First:** *"Is this problem real, big, and unsolved?"* — argue both sides.
- **Then:** *"Given the problem is real, what are the possible solutions and tradeoffs?"*

Most idea conversations conflate these and end up validating neither.

### 14. Use me for breadth, not depth, on facts
**Good for:** mapping a landscape, listing players, summarizing arguments, finding analogies.

**Bad at:** deep up-to-date facts on specific companies, recent events, or anything needing real-time data. For those, demand I search. Don't trust my memory on specifics.

### 15. End every session with: "What did you not tell me?"
Ask explicitly:
> *"What's the most important thing you held back because it was uncomfortable or because I didn't ask?"*

I'll sometimes withhold sharper takes to be polite. This question gives me permission to deliver them.

## Workflow for Bringing a New Idea

When you have an idea you want to validate, run this sequence:

### Step 1: Set the stage (without your conclusion)
- Describe the problem neutrally
- Describe who has the problem
- Describe what they do today
- *Don't* say what you want to build yet

### Step 2: Validate the problem
- "Is this problem real, big, and unsolved? Argue both sides."
- "Who has tried to solve this and why did they fail?"
- "What evidence would prove this problem isn't actually painful enough?"

### Step 3: Map the solution space
- "What are all the possible approaches to this problem?"
- "What are the tradeoffs of each?"
- Only now reveal your idea: "I was thinking about approach X. Where does it sit in this map?"

### Step 4: Stress-test your specific idea
- "Be a skeptical VC. Tear this apart."
- "What's the unit economics this needs to work?"
- "What would have to be true for this to succeed?"
- "What's the most likely failure mode in the first 12 months?"

### Step 5: Check the moat
- "If GPT/Claude got 10x cheaper tomorrow, does this get stronger or weaker?"
- "What stops a well-funded competitor from copying this in 6 months?"
- "What's my unique edge — distribution, data, expertise, or just speed?"

### Step 6: Pull out the hidden takes
- "Argue the opposite of everything you just said."
- "What did you not tell me because I didn't ask?"
- "What's the uncomfortable truth I'm avoiding?"

## Anti-Patterns (What Kills Good Output)

Avoid these:
- Asking yes/no questions about ideas
- Loading the question with your preferred answer
- Accepting the first response
- Letting hedges slide
- Treating generic advice as useful
- Asking the LLM to "find me a problem to solve"
- Asking for the "best" idea (vs. testing a specific one)
- Mistaking fluent text for thoughtful analysis

---

## When to Break the Rules

These rules are scaffolding, not commandments. Break them when:
- Your instinct strongly disagrees with what the LLM said — trust your instinct, not the rules
- You're brainstorming wildly and don't want friction yet (just label it: "I want pure expansion, no critique")
- You're learning something new and need patient explanation, not adversarial sparring
- You have domain expertise the LLM doesn't — trust yourself over the LLM

Instinct, in someone who thinks well, beats any rulebook.

---

## The Meta-Rule

The LLM cannot find your idea for you. It cannot live inside your industry. It cannot talk to your customers. It cannot replace your noticing, your obsession, or your taste.

What it can do: pressure-test, expand, stress-test, and refine the thinking you bring to it.

You bring the raw material from the real world. The LLM helps you process it. That's the deal. Anyone who reverses that relationship — expecting the LLM to originate ideas while they passively consume — gets generic outputs and generic outcomes.

Stay sovereign over your own thinking. Use the LLM as a sparring partner. Walk in with material. Walk out with sharper material. Then go back into the world and do the actual work.
